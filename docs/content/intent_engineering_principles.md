### Intent Engineering Principles

| Advice Category | Principle | Why This Works | What It Is | Limitations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Level 1: Defining Purpose** | **Define the End Goal** | An AI without a clear end goal will generate output that looks productive but may not serve your actual need. Telling the AI what you're trying to produce gives it the orientation to make good decisions about scope, depth, and format throughout the task. | Tell the AI explicitly what the final deliverable is: "I need a two-page executive summary," "I need a ranked list of the five strongest candidates," "I need a decision matrix comparing these three vendors." When the AI knows the destination, it can make better judgments about what to include, what to skip, and when it's done. Without this, it defaults to generating as much as possible about the topic, which wastes tokens and buries what matters. | Users sometimes communicate the process ("use AI to analyze this data") rather than the outcome ("I need a summary of the three key trends"). If your goal sounds like a method rather than a deliverable, reframe it. |
| **Level 1: Defining Purpose** | **Identify Accountability Requirements** | Telling the AI who the output is for and what standards apply changes how it approaches the work. An AI drafting an internal brainstorm should behave differently than one drafting a regulatory submission. Without this context, the AI has no way to calibrate its level of care, precision, or conservatism. | Communicate the stakes and audience directly: "This will be reviewed by our legal team," "This is an internal draft for discussion only," "This goes to the grant review panel." When the AI knows a regulator or board will see the output, it can prioritize precision and hedging over creativity. When it knows the work is exploratory, it can be more generative. The AI can't make these trade-offs if it doesn't know who's accountable for the result. | Accountability can be diffuse in organizations, with multiple stakeholders sharing responsibility. When the audience is unclear, tell the AI to assume the higher-stakes scenario. It's easier to loosen up a careful draft than to add rigor to a casual one. |
| **Level 1: Defining Purpose** | **Establish Success Criteria** | Telling the AI what "good enough" looks like is how you communicate constraints and boundaries. "Make this compelling" is a very different instruction than "make this accurate above all else." Without success criteria in your intent, the AI optimizes for whatever it assumes you want. | Include 2-3 measurable or observable criteria directly in your prompt: accuracy thresholds, completeness requirements, tone expectations, or format specifications. "Summarize this report" gives the AI no guardrails. "Summarize this report prioritizing factual accuracy over readability, covering all five budget categories, in under 300 words" tells the AI exactly what trade-offs to make. The criteria aren't just for you to check later; they shape what the AI produces in the first place. | Success criteria can be hard to define for creative or exploratory tasks. In those cases, define what failure looks like instead; that's often easier and communicates boundaries just as effectively. |
| **Level 2: Escape Hatches** | **Tell the AI When to Stop** | AI will pursue task completion relentlessly unless you give it explicit reasons to pause or halt. Without stop conditions in your intent, the AI has no way to know that completing the task the wrong way is worse than not completing it at all. | Include explicit stopping conditions in your instructions: "If you can't verify a claim, flag it instead of guessing," "If this requires access to data you don't have, say so rather than inferring," "Stop and ask before taking any action that would be difficult to undo." These aren't suggestions to the AI; they're guardrails that give it permission to prioritize safety over completion. Example: "Draft a budget summary from this spreadsheet. If any numbers don't match between tabs, stop and flag the discrepancy rather than choosing one." | Stop conditions work best when they're specific and concrete. Vague instructions like "be careful" don't give the AI actionable criteria for when to halt. The more precisely you define the trigger, the more reliably the AI will respect it. |
| **Level 2: Escape Hatches** | **Define Human Handoff Points** | AI should know where its authority ends and a human's begins. Without explicit handoff instructions, the AI will make judgment calls it isn't qualified to make, because its only directive is to complete the task. | Tell the AI exactly where to stop and defer: "Draft the recommendation but do not send it," "Identify the top three candidates but do not rank them," "Flag potential compliance issues but do not interpret the regulation." Common handoff points: before external communications, before financial commitments, before anything with legal implications, and before any action that affects people's evaluations or standing. | Too many handoff points can make the AI's output so fragmented it's not useful. Focus on the junctures where an AI misjudgment would be costly or irreversible, and let the AI work autonomously on everything else. |
| **Level 2: Escape Hatches** | **Set Boundaries** | Telling the AI what NOT to do is often more important than telling it what to do. Without explicit boundaries, AI optimizes for task completion and may take shortcuts you never intended: accessing restricted information, making assumptions about sensitive topics, or overstepping its role. The AI doesn't know your constraints unless you state them. | Write exclusions directly into your prompt or system instructions: "Do not access files outside this directory," "Do not draft language that implies institutional commitment," "Do not process personally identifiable information for this task," "Do not use web scraping to gather this data." Think about both WHAT the AI should not do and HOW it should not accomplish its goals. A boundary like "find the answer but do not contact anyone" constrains the method, not just the output. | Boundaries are hard to anticipate fully before you see the AI in action. Start with the obvious exclusions and refine as you observe where the AI overreaches. The first run often reveals boundary gaps you didn't think of. |
| **Level 2: Escape Hatches** | **Give the AI Permission to Flag Uncertainty** | By default, AI produces confident-sounding output whether it's certain or guessing. If you don't explicitly tell the AI it's allowed (or expected) to express doubt, it will fill gaps with plausible-sounding fabrications rather than admitting what it doesn't know. This is one of the most direct ways to reduce hallucinations: an AI that has permission to say "I don't know" doesn't need to invent an answer. | Include instructions like: "If you're unsure about any claim, mark it as unverified," "Distinguish between what the source document says and what you're inferring," "If the data is ambiguous, present both interpretations rather than picking one." This gives the AI a sanctioned alternative to guessing. Example: "Summarize the findings from this report. Where the report is ambiguous or you're uncertain about the interpretation, note it explicitly rather than presenting your best guess as fact." | Even with explicit permission, AI models may still under-report uncertainty due to training incentives toward confident, helpful responses. Treat flagged uncertainty as a minimum bound, not a complete accounting of what the AI doesn't know. |
| **Level 3: Ethical Considerations** | **Assess Bias Risk** | AI systems can introduce or amplify unfair outcomes, especially in tasks involving people, evaluations, or resource allocation. Checking for this upfront prevents harm. | Ask: "Could this AI output disadvantage any group or individual unfairly?" Consider whether training data biases could affect results - e.g., in resume screening, grant scoring, performance evaluations, or content targeting specific demographics. | Bias is often invisible until it causes harm, and not all biases are detectable through simple review. For high-impact decisions, pair AI outputs with diverse human reviewers rather than relying on self-assessment alone. |
| **Level 3: Ethical Considerations** | **Evaluate Appropriateness** | Not every task should involve AI, even if it technically can. Some contexts demand human-only processes for trust, sensitivity, or regulatory reasons. | Ask: "Would the people affected by this output be comfortable knowing AI was involved?" Consider tasks like delivering bad news, counseling, handling grievances, or interpreting sensitive cultural material. If the answer is uncertain, default to human-led processes. | Appropriateness is context-dependent and culturally variable. What's acceptable in one organization or community may not be in another. When norms are unclear, consult stakeholders before proceeding. |
| **Level 3: Ethical Considerations** | **Consider Stakeholder Impact** | Ensures you think beyond your immediate needs to the broader consequences of using AI for a given task. | List everyone affected by the output: end users, colleagues, clients, communities. For each, ask: "Does this AI use respect their interests, privacy, and autonomy?" If any stakeholder would be surprised or harmed, reconsider or add safeguards. | Stakeholder analysis can become an endless exercise. Prioritize the most directly affected groups and the most vulnerable populations rather than trying to map every possible impact. |
| **Level 4: Meta-Intent** | **Reflect on Intent Completeness** | Catches blind spots by forcing a final review of your intent before you begin - the questions you didn't think to ask are often the most important ones. | Before starting, review your intent checklist: Do I know the goal? The audience? Who's accountable? What's the fallback? Where are the ethical risks? What are the boundaries? If any answer is missing or vague, pause and clarify before proceeding. | Checklists can create false confidence - completing every item doesn't guarantee you've covered every risk. Use the checklist as a prompt for deeper thinking, not as a substitute for judgment. |
| **Level 4: Meta-Intent** | **Revisit Intent as Context Changes** | Intent established at the start of a project may no longer hold as requirements shift, new information emerges, or stakeholders change their minds. Periodic reassessment prevents drift. | Schedule intent check-ins at natural milestones: after receiving initial AI output, when scope changes, when new stakeholders join, or when the deadline shifts. Ask: "Does my original intent still match what I actually need? Have the stakes changed?" | Frequent reassessment can slow momentum and create decision fatigue. Reserve formal check-ins for meaningful context shifts rather than every minor change. |
