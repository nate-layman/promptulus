### Context Engineering Principles

| Advice Category | Principle | Why This Works | What It Is | Limitations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Level 1: Information Selection** | **Identify What the AI Doesn't Know** | AI models have broad but shallow knowledge; they lack your institution's specific policies, recent data, internal jargon, and unpublished work. Identifying these gaps prevents hallucination. | Before prompting, ask: "What does the AI need to see that it couldn't possibly already know?" This includes internal documents, recent policy changes, proprietary data, organizational terminology, and anything not publicly available. If you don't provide it, the AI will guess, or make something up. | Users sometimes over-correct by providing everything, burying the critical context in noise. Focus on what's essential and unique to your situation. |
| **Level 1: Information Selection** | **Match Context to Task Scope** | Providing too much context overwhelms the model and dilutes its attention; too little leaves it guessing. The right amount depends on what you're actually asking. | For a narrow question ("What's the deadline for this grant?"), provide just the relevant section of the document. For a broad task ("Summarize this policy"), provide the full text. Match the volume and specificity of context to the scope of the request. | Judging the right scope requires experience. When in doubt, start with more context and trim based on output quality. |
| **Level 1: Information Selection** | **Prioritize Authoritative Sources** | AI treats all context equally. It doesn't know which document is the official policy and which is a draft from three years ago. You must curate for authority. | When providing context, include only the most current, authoritative version of each document. Label sources explicitly: "This is the current 2024 policy" or "This is the official guidelines from the sponsor." Remove outdated drafts that could confuse the model. | In organizations with many document versions, finding the authoritative source can itself be a challenge. When uncertain, note the version and date so the AI can flag ambiguity. |
| **Level 2: Organization & Formatting** | **Structure Context for Scanning** | AI models process context sequentially and attend more to well-structured input. Clear formatting helps the model find and use the right information. | Use headings, labels, and separators to organize your context: "### Policy Text", "### Key Requirements", "### Examples". Put the most important information first. Use bullet points for lists and tables for structured data. | Over-formatting simple inputs adds unnecessary complexity. Match the level of structure to the complexity of the context. |
| **Level 2: Organization & Formatting** | **Separate Instructions from Data** | When instructions and data are mixed together, the model may confuse what to do with what to analyze. Clear separation prevents misinterpretation. | Use explicit labels like "INSTRUCTIONS:" and "DOCUMENT TO ANALYZE:" or formatting markers like --- to separate your request from the reference material. This helps the model understand its role versus the content it should work with. | For very short prompts with minimal context, formal separation can feel heavy-handed. Use your judgment; the principle matters most when context is long or complex. |
| **Level 2: Organization & Formatting** | **Use Examples as Context** | Concrete examples teach the model what you want more effectively than abstract instructions alone. They serve as implicit formatting and quality guides. | Include 1-3 examples of the desired output alongside your instructions. For instance, if you want meeting summaries in a specific format, include a sample summary. The model will pattern-match against your examples. | Examples can anchor the model too strongly. It may copy the example's style even when the content calls for something different. Vary your examples to avoid over-fitting. |
| **Level 2: Organization & Formatting** | **Give the AI a Template to Fill In** | When you show the AI the exact structure you want (with blanks where the content goes), it produces output that matches your format on the first try instead of guessing. | Create a skeleton of your desired output with placeholders and include it in your prompt. For example, if you need a project summary, provide: "Project Title: ___, PI: ___, Budget: $___,  Objectives: 1. ___ 2. ___ 3. ___". The AI fills in the blanks using the context you've provided, giving you consistent, predictable formatting every time. | Templates work best for structured, repeatable outputs. For creative or exploratory tasks, a rigid template may constrain the AI's most useful contributions. Start with a loose template and tighten it as you learn what format works best. |
| **Level 2: Organization & Formatting** | **Ground the AI in Real Documents** | The biggest source of AI errors is letting it work from memory instead of from your actual data. When AI has direct access to the real documents, it quotes and references what's there instead of guessing what might be there. | Whenever possible, give the AI direct access to your source material rather than describing it from memory. Copy-paste the actual policy text, the real spreadsheet data, or the specific email thread. Better yet, many AI tools now let you upload files, connect to document libraries, or search your organization's knowledge base during the conversation. These features exist specifically to ground the AI in facts rather than letting it improvise. If your tool supports file uploads, document connections, or search integrations, use them. | Providing raw documents works well for small amounts of text, but organizations with large document libraries need more systematic approaches. As your AI use matures, look into tools that can automatically pull the right documents into context. Your IT team may call these "RAG systems" or "knowledge bases." The key insight is simple: the more real data the AI can see, the less it needs to guess. |
| **Level 3: Window Management** | **Respect the Page Limit** | Every AI model has a finite context window, a "page limit." Everything you include (instructions, documents, conversation history, examples) competes for space. Exceeding it means the model silently drops information. | Before loading context, estimate how much space you have. A typical context window holds roughly 50-200 pages of text depending on the model. If your documents exceed this, you must prioritize. Ask: "If I could only bring 10 pages into the room, which 10 would matter most?" | Context window sizes vary by model and are growing rapidly. What's too large today may fit easily tomorrow. But the principle of prioritization remains. More context always means more competition for attention. |
| **Level 3: Window Management** | **Front-Load Critical Information** | AI models pay more consistent attention to information that appears early in the context. Important details buried deep may be overlooked. | Put your most important context (key requirements, critical constraints, essential data) at the beginning. Save supporting details, background, and nice-to-have context for later. Think of it like a newspaper: lead with the headline. | This interacts with how you structure your prompt. If your instructions come first and context second, the model may give instructions more weight than the data. Experiment with ordering. |
| **Level 3: Window Management** | **Beware the Middle of the Stack** | Research consistently shows that AI models pay the most attention to information near the beginning and end of the context window, and the least attention to what's in the middle. This is sometimes called the "lost in the middle" effect. The longer the context, the worse the problem gets. | Imagine handing someone a thick stack of papers and asking a question. They'll read the first few pages carefully, skim the middle, and read the last few pages carefully. AI models behave similarly. If your most critical information lands in the middle of a long prompt - sandwiched between setup context and trailing instructions - the model may underweight or overlook it entirely. The practical fix: put your most important context near the top or the bottom, not buried in the middle. If you're providing multiple documents, put the most critical one first or last, not second of four. | This effect varies by model and is an active area of research. Some newer models handle it better than others, and the severity depends on how long the overall context is. For short prompts, positioning matters much less. But as your context grows - especially past a few thousand words - information placement becomes a real factor in output quality. When the AI misses something you know you included, check where it appeared in the prompt before assuming the model can't handle the task. |
| **Level 3: Window Management** | **Summarize When You Can't Include Everything** | When the full document won't fit, a good summary preserves the essential information while freeing space for other context. | Instead of cramming in a full 50-page report, write a 1-2 paragraph summary of its key findings and include that. Or ask the AI to summarize the document first, then use that summary as context for your actual task. This is "context compression." | Summaries inevitably lose detail. If precision matters (exact numbers, specific language, legal terms), include the relevant excerpts verbatim rather than summarizing. |
| **Level 3: Window Management** | **Know What Never Goes In** | Some information should never be pasted into an AI tool, regardless of how useful it might be as context. Once data enters a prompt, you've shared it with the AI provider, and you may not be able to take it back. | Before providing context, screen for: **Personally identifiable information (PII)**: names, Social Security numbers, student IDs, medical records. **Credentials**: passwords, API keys, access tokens. **Regulated data**: anything covered by FERPA, HIPAA, ITAR, or your institution's data classification policies. **Confidential research**: unpublished findings, proprietary methods, trade secrets. When in doubt, redact or anonymize before pasting. Replace real names with "Person A," swap exact IDs with placeholders, and remove anything you wouldn't put in an email to an external contact. | The line between "safe to share" and "sensitive" isn't always obvious. Your institution's data classification policy is the authority. If you don't know the policy, that's your first step. Some AI tools offer enterprise agreements with stronger data protections, but "enterprise" doesn't mean "anything goes." Check what's actually covered. |
| **Level 4: Institutional Knowledge** | **Build Reusable Context Blocks** | Many tasks in an organization use the same background information: policies, templates, terminology guides. Preparing these once saves time and improves consistency. | Create a library of context blocks: a paragraph explaining your institution's grant policies, a template for how you format reports, a glossary of internal acronyms. Paste the relevant block into each prompt instead of rewriting from scratch. | Context blocks go stale. Policies change, terminology evolves. Review and update your blocks periodically, especially after major policy revisions. |
| **Level 5: Meta-Context** | **Audit Your Context Strategy** | What you include shapes what the AI produces. Periodically reviewing your context choices reveals blind spots, outdated information, or missed opportunities. | After getting AI output, ask: "Did the AI have everything it needed? Did it use information I wish I hadn't included? Would different context have produced a better result?" Use this reflection to improve your next attempt. | Context auditing requires comparing output quality across different context configurations, which can be time-consuming. Focus on high-impact tasks where context quality matters most. |
| **Level 5: Meta-Context** | **Evolve Context as the Task Evolves** | Tasks rarely stay static. Requirements change, new information emerges, and what the AI needs to know shifts over time. Static context leads to stale results. | At natural milestones (after initial output, when requirements change, when new data arrives), revisit your context. Ask: "Is everything here still relevant? Is anything missing that wasn't available before?" Update your context blocks and re-run if needed. | Constant context revision can slow down fast-moving work. Balance thoroughness with pragmatism. Not every update requires a full context audit. |
