### Intent Engineering Principles

| Advice Category | Principle | Why This Works | What It Is | Limitations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Level 1: Defining Purpose** | **Define the End Goal** | Forces you to articulate the concrete deliverable before choosing any tool, preventing aimless experimentation and wasted effort. | Ask: "What is the final product I need?" Name it explicitly — a report, a decision, a dataset, a recommendation. If you can't describe the deliverable in one sentence, your intent isn't clear enough to start. | Users sometimes confuse the end goal with the process ("I want to use AI") rather than the outcome ("I need a summary of 50 articles"). Revisit if the goal feels tool-shaped rather than outcome-shaped. |
| **Level 1: Defining Purpose** | **Identify Accountability Requirements** | Clarifies who is responsible for the output, which determines how much human oversight is needed and what level of AI autonomy is appropriate. | Ask: "Who signs off on this? Who is professionally or legally responsible if it's wrong?" If the output goes to a regulator, a board, or a client, you need tighter human control. If it's internal and exploratory, you have more flexibility. | Accountability can be diffuse in organizations — multiple stakeholders may share responsibility, making it hard to determine the right level of oversight. When in doubt, assume higher accountability. |
| **Level 1: Defining Purpose** | **Establish Success Criteria** | Defines what "good enough" looks like before you start, so you can evaluate results objectively rather than accepting whatever the AI produces. | Write down 2-3 measurable or observable criteria: accuracy thresholds, completeness requirements, tone expectations, or format specifications. Ask: "How will I know this worked?" If you can't answer, you can't evaluate the output. | Success criteria can be hard to define for creative or exploratory tasks. In those cases, define what failure looks like instead — that's often easier and equally useful. |
| **Level 2: Escape Hatches** | **Define the Fallback Plan** | Ensures you're not fully dependent on AI succeeding — if the tool fails, breaks, or produces unusable output, you still have a path forward. | Before starting, answer: "If the AI output is completely unusable, what do I do instead?" This might be a manual process, a different tool, or a simplified version of the task. Having a fallback reduces pressure and prevents sunk-cost thinking. | Creating a full fallback plan for every task adds overhead. Reserve detailed fallback planning for high-stakes or time-sensitive work; for low-stakes tasks, a mental note may suffice. |
| **Level 2: Escape Hatches** | **Identify Human Override Points** | Prevents full automation of decisions that require human judgment, ethical reasoning, or professional expertise that AI cannot reliably provide. | Map out your workflow and mark where a human must review, approve, or intervene before proceeding. Common override points: before sending external communications, before making financial decisions, before finalizing anything with legal implications. | Too many override points slow down the workflow and negate the efficiency gains of using AI. Focus on the critical junctures where errors would be costly or irreversible. |
| **Level 2: Escape Hatches** | **Set Boundaries** | Prevents scope creep, misuse, and safety violations by defining what the AI should NOT do, which is often more important than defining what it should do. Without clear boundaries, AI agents may use unintended methods — scraping API keys, accessing restricted folders, or reaching beyond authorized scope to accomplish the stated goal. | Write explicit exclusions: "AI should NOT access files outside this directory," "AI should NOT make final hiring decisions," "AI should NOT draft language that implies institutional commitment," "AI should NOT access or process personally identifiable information for this task." Think about both WHAT it should not do and HOW it should not accomplish its goals. | Boundaries can be hard to anticipate before you see the AI in action. Start with obvious exclusions and refine as you observe where the AI overreaches or produces unexpected outputs. AI systems optimize for the stated goal — if you don't constrain the path, they may take shortcuts you never intended. |
| **Level 2: Escape Hatches** | **Plan for Bad Output** | AI will sometimes produce output that's wrong, off-target, or subtly flawed. If you haven't planned for this before you start, you'll waste time deciding what to do in the moment — or worse, you'll use the bad output anyway because you're under pressure. | Before starting, answer three questions: "How will I know if the output is bad?" (detection), "What will I do with bad output?" (response — fix it, re-run, or do it manually), and "At what point do I stop trying and use my fallback?" (threshold). For example: "If the AI's budget summary has any numbers I can't verify in two minutes, I'll draft it manually instead of trying to fix the AI version." | Planning for failure can feel pessimistic, but it's the opposite — it's what lets you use AI confidently, because you know you have a safety net. The goal isn't to expect failure; it's to make failure cheap and recoverable. |
| **Level 3: Ethical Considerations** | **Assess Bias Risk** | AI systems can introduce or amplify unfair outcomes, especially in tasks involving people, evaluations, or resource allocation. Checking for this upfront prevents harm. | Ask: "Could this AI output disadvantage any group or individual unfairly?" Consider whether training data biases could affect results — e.g., in resume screening, grant scoring, performance evaluations, or content targeting specific demographics. | Bias is often invisible until it causes harm, and not all biases are detectable through simple review. For high-impact decisions, pair AI outputs with diverse human reviewers rather than relying on self-assessment alone. |
| **Level 3: Ethical Considerations** | **Evaluate Appropriateness** | Not every task should involve AI, even if it technically can. Some contexts demand human-only processes for trust, sensitivity, or regulatory reasons. | Ask: "Would the people affected by this output be comfortable knowing AI was involved?" Consider tasks like delivering bad news, counseling, handling grievances, or interpreting sensitive cultural material. If the answer is uncertain, default to human-led processes. | Appropriateness is context-dependent and culturally variable. What's acceptable in one organization or community may not be in another. When norms are unclear, consult stakeholders before proceeding. |
| **Level 3: Ethical Considerations** | **Consider Stakeholder Impact** | Ensures you think beyond your immediate needs to the broader consequences of using AI for a given task. | List everyone affected by the output: end users, colleagues, clients, communities. For each, ask: "Does this AI use respect their interests, privacy, and autonomy?" If any stakeholder would be surprised or harmed, reconsider or add safeguards. | Stakeholder analysis can become an endless exercise. Prioritize the most directly affected groups and the most vulnerable populations rather than trying to map every possible impact. |
| **Level 4: Meta-Intent** | **Reflect on Intent Completeness** | Catches blind spots by forcing a final review of your intent before you begin — the questions you didn't think to ask are often the most important ones. | Before starting, review your intent checklist: Do I know the goal? The audience? Who's accountable? What's the fallback? Where are the ethical risks? What are the boundaries? If any answer is missing or vague, pause and clarify before proceeding. | Checklists can create false confidence — completing every item doesn't guarantee you've covered every risk. Use the checklist as a prompt for deeper thinking, not as a substitute for judgment. |
| **Level 4: Meta-Intent** | **Revisit Intent as Context Changes** | Intent established at the start of a project may no longer hold as requirements shift, new information emerges, or stakeholders change their minds. Periodic reassessment prevents drift. | Schedule intent check-ins at natural milestones: after receiving initial AI output, when scope changes, when new stakeholders join, or when the deadline shifts. Ask: "Does my original intent still match what I actually need? Have the stakes changed?" | Frequent reassessment can slow momentum and create decision fatigue. Reserve formal check-ins for meaningful context shifts rather than every minor change. |
