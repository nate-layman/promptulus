### Conversation Management Principles

| Advice Category | Principle | Why This Works | What It Is | Limitations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Level 1: Conversation Awareness** | **Understand the Shared Notebook** | Every message in a conversation (yours and the AI's) stays in the context window. The AI reads the entire history before each response. Understanding this changes how you structure interactions. | Think of each conversation as a notebook that both you and the AI write in. The AI re-reads the entire notebook before every response. This means early messages influence late responses, old mistakes persist, and the notebook has a page limit. Manage accordingly. | Users often forget that AI responses also consume context space. A verbose early response can crowd out later, more important context. |
| **Level 1: Conversation Awareness** | **Recognize Conversation Drift** | Over multiple turns, conversations naturally drift from the original topic or intent. The AI follows wherever the conversation leads, even if that's away from your goal. | Watch for signs of drift: the AI starts addressing topics you didn't ask about, responses become less focused, or you find yourself clarifying things that were clear earlier. Drift often starts subtly. One tangent leads to another until the conversation is far from where you started. | Some drift is productive. Exploration and tangents can lead to insights. The key is recognizing when drift is helping versus when it's degrading output quality. |
| **Level 1: Conversation Awareness** | **Track Your Instruction History** | When you give new instructions that contradict earlier ones, the AI must choose which to follow, and it doesn't always choose the latest. Accumulated contradictions degrade response quality. | Before sending a new instruction, ask: "Have I already told the AI something different about this?" If you've changed your requirements, format expectations, or constraints, the old instructions are still in the notebook competing with the new ones. Acknowledge the change explicitly: "Ignore my earlier request for bullet points; I now want paragraphs." | Tracking every instruction across a long conversation is mentally taxing. For complex tasks, consider using a summary turn to restate all current requirements. |
| **Level 2: Active Management** | **Summarize Before Continuing** | Long conversations accumulate noise: tangents, corrections, superseded instructions. A mid-conversation summary refocuses the AI on what actually matters now. | When a conversation exceeds 5-10 turns, pause and write a summary turn: "Here's where we are: [current goal], [key decisions made], [what I need next]." This effectively resets the AI's focus without losing the work you've done. | Summaries take effort to write well. A poor summary can itself introduce confusion. Be precise about what's current and what's been superseded. |
| **Level 2: Active Management** | **Use Explicit Turn Signals** | Without clear signals, the AI may not know whether you want it to continue, change direction, or wait for more input. Explicit signals prevent misinterpretation. | Tell the AI what you expect from the next turn: "Now revise the second paragraph," "Let's switch to a different approach," "Hold on, I want to add more context before you respond." These signals help the AI understand your intent for each exchange. | Over-signaling can make conversations feel robotic. Use explicit signals when the transition isn't obvious. You don't need them for straightforward follow-ups. |
| **Level 2: Active Management** | **Correct Early, Not Late** | Errors in early turns compound throughout the conversation. The AI builds on its own responses, so a mistake in turn 2 infects turns 3, 4, and beyond. | If you spot an error or misunderstanding in the AI's response, correct it immediately in your next turn. Don't wait and hope it self-corrects. Say: "That's not quite right: [correct information]. Please use this going forward." The sooner you correct, the less cleanup you need later. | Sometimes errors aren't obvious until several turns later. When you discover a deep-rooted error, it may be more efficient to start fresh than to try correcting the accumulated damage. |
| **Level 3: Strategic Decisions** | **Know When to Start Fresh** | Sometimes a conversation is so muddled with contradictions, tangents, and corrections that continuing costs more than restarting. A fresh conversation with clear context often outperforms a long, messy one. | Consider starting fresh when: you've changed requirements more than twice, the AI keeps referencing outdated instructions, responses are getting worse despite corrections, or you've lost track of what the AI "knows." Copy your latest, clearest request into a new conversation. | Starting fresh means losing useful context from the current conversation. Before resetting, extract any valuable outputs or decisions you want to preserve. |
| **Level 3: Strategic Decisions** | **Choose Single-Turn vs Multi-Turn Deliberately** | Not every task benefits from conversation. Some tasks are better as standalone prompts where you provide everything upfront. Others genuinely need back-and-forth. | Ask: "Does this task need iteration and refinement, or do I know exactly what I want?" If you can write a complete, self-contained prompt, do that. It avoids all the complexity of conversation management. Reserve multi-turn for tasks that genuinely benefit from exploration, refinement, or building on previous outputs. | Users default to multi-turn when single-turn would suffice, partly because chatting feels natural. But each additional turn adds context that may help or hurt. There's no free lunch. |
| **Level 3: Strategic Decisions** | **Split Conversations by Subtask** | When a conversation covers multiple distinct subtasks, the context for each subtask competes for the AI's attention. Separate conversations for separate purposes produce cleaner results. | If your project has distinct phases (research, drafting, reviewing), consider using a separate conversation for each. Pass only the essential output from one conversation to the next. This keeps each conversation focused and its context relevant. | Managing multiple conversations adds coordination overhead. Use this approach for complex, multi-phase work, not for simple tasks that fit naturally in one exchange. |
| **Level 4: Conversation Design** | **Design Conversation Templates** | For recurring tasks, a pre-planned conversation structure saves time and produces more consistent results than improvising each time. | Create a template for conversations you repeat: "Turn 1: provide context and constraints. Turn 2: ask for first draft. Turn 3: request specific revisions. Turn 4: finalize." Having a plan prevents drift and ensures you cover all necessary steps. | Templates can feel rigid for creative or exploratory work. Use them as starting points, not straitjackets. Adapt as the conversation unfolds. |
| **Level 4: Conversation Design** | **Build in Checkpoints** | Planned checkpoints let you evaluate whether the conversation is on track before investing more turns. They prevent the sunk-cost trap of continuing a failing conversation. | At planned intervals (every 3-5 turns, or at phase transitions), pause and ask: "Is the conversation still serving my goal? Is the AI's understanding aligned with mine? Should I continue, summarize, or start fresh?" Make this evaluation a habit, not an afterthought. | Checkpoints add overhead to quick tasks. Reserve them for conversations longer than 5 turns or tasks where output quality matters significantly. |
| **Level 5: Meta-Conversation** | **Reflect on Conversation Patterns** | Understanding your own conversation habits reveals systematic issues: do you tend to give too many instructions at once? Change direction too often? Continue conversations too long? | After completing a task, review the conversation: "Where did it go well? Where did it break down? What would I do differently next time?" Look for patterns across multiple conversations. Common issues: instruction overload, delayed corrections, failure to summarize, and reluctance to start fresh. | Self-reflection requires honest assessment and takes time. Focus on conversations where the outcome was notably good or bad. Those reveal the most about your habits. |
| **Level 5: Meta-Conversation** | **Teach Others Your Conversation Strategy** | If you can explain your conversation management approach to a colleague, you understand it well enough to use it consistently. Teaching also reveals gaps in your own understanding. | Document your conversation strategies: when you start fresh, how you structure multi-turn tasks, what signals you use, and what checkpoints you build in. Share these with your team. The act of documenting forces clarity, and others benefit from your experience. | Conversation strategies are somewhat personal. What works for one person's communication style may not work for another's. Share strategies as suggestions, not mandates. |
