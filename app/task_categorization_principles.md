### Task Assessment Principles

| Advice Category | Principle | Why This Works | What It Is | Limitations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Level 1: Audit & Attestation** | **AI Has No Integrity** | The authority of professional work comes from the integrity of the person behind it. A research administrator's recommendation carries weight because they stake their professional reputation on it. A compliance officer's sign-off means something because they're accountable. AI has none of this. | Ask: "Does this task require someone to stand behind the result with their professional judgment?" AI will confidently produce wrong answers without consequence. It has no reputation, no ethics, and no skin in the game. It can inform and accelerate your work, but the final call on any decision must belong to someone with the integrity to own it. Even for low-transparency tasks, someone should be able to say: "I reviewed this and it's correct." | This doesn't mean AI is useless, far from it. But it means AI is a tool in your hands, not a colleague with professional obligations. The distinction matters most when something goes wrong: a human can be held accountable and learn from it. AI just generates the next response. |
| **Level 1: Audit & Attestation** | **Who Needs to See the Work?** | If someone (a sponsor, auditor, or compliance officer) needs to trace how the work was done, you need tools that show their steps. AI can't provide that paper trail. | Ask: "Could someone ask me to show exactly how I arrived at this result?" If the answer is yes, you need transparent, auditable methods. A spreadsheet formula can be inspected; an AI-generated answer cannot. Examples: effort certification, cost sharing calculations, institutional assurances. | Not all "show your work" tasks are equally strict. Internal reviews may accept less documentation than federal audits. |
| **Level 1: Audit & Attestation** | **Is This Auditable?** | Tasks that could be audited need methods that produce the same result every time and leave a trail. AI outputs aren't reproducible. Ask the same question twice, get different answers. | Ask: "If an auditor asked how this was done, could I show them?" If you need to demonstrate your methodology, use tools where every step is visible. Spreadsheets, databases, and documented procedures pass this test. AI does not. | Some audits focus on outcomes rather than methods. A final report may be audited for accuracy without anyone asking how it was drafted. |
| **Level 1: Audit & Attestation** | **Does Someone Attest to the Process?** | When a specific person must certify that the work was done correctly (not just that the result looks right), the process itself is the deliverable. | Ask: "Does anyone need to personally vouch for how this was completed?" If a PI certifies effort, a compliance officer signs off on a protocol, or a financial officer approves a reconciliation, those people are attesting to the process. They need to understand every step. | Attestation requirements vary by institution and sponsor. What requires sign-off at one university may be delegated at another. |
| **Level 2: Explainability** | **Can You Show Your Work?** | If you need to explain your method to a colleague, supervisor, or sponsor, you need a method you actually understand. You can explain a spreadsheet formula. You cannot meaningfully explain what an AI model did internally. | Ask: "If someone asked 'how did you get this number?' could I give a clear answer?" If the answer is "I asked AI and it told me," consider whether that's acceptable for this task. For internal brainstorming, maybe. For a sponsor deliverable, probably not. | Explainability is audience-dependent. "I used AI to draft it and then verified the facts" may satisfy some stakeholders but not others. |
| **Level 2: Explainability** | **Does the Method Need to Be Reproducible?** | If running the same task again should produce the same result, AI is unreliable. It generates different outputs each time. Deterministic tools (formulas, documented procedures) are reproducible. | Ask: "If I ran this again tomorrow with the same inputs, would I need the same answer?" Budget calculations, compliance checks, and financial reconciliations need reproducibility. First drafts, brainstorming, and exploratory summaries tolerate variation. | Some tasks need approximate consistency rather than exact reproducibility. A budget narrative that's "roughly the same" each time may be fine. |
| **Level 2: Explainability** | **Would a Reviewer Accept "AI Did It"?** | This is a practical litmus test. If saying "I used AI for this" would make a reviewer pause, that's a signal the task needs more transparency. | Imagine submitting the work product and being asked about your methodology. Would "I used AI" be a satisfying answer? For formatting a bibliography: probably fine. For calculating cost sharing: probably not. Let the expected reaction guide your approach. | Reviewer expectations are evolving. What raises eyebrows today may be routine tomorrow. But it's safer to match current expectations than to assume future acceptance. |
| **Level 3: Process vs Output** | **Does It Matter How?** | This is the central question. If anyone cares about the method (not just the result), the task needs a transparent approach. If only the final product matters, AI can take the lead. | Ask: "Does anyone care how this work got done, or only that the final product is correct?" If the process matters, it's process critical. If only the output matters, it's output-driven. If the process matters somewhat, it's human-in-the-loop. | Many tasks feel output-driven but have hidden process requirements. A facilities description "just needs to be accurate," but if it's in a federal proposal, someone may ask how you verified those facts. |
| **Level 3: Process vs Output** | **Is the Journey or the Destination What Counts?** | Another way to frame the same question. For some tasks, the work itself is the point (a PI reviewing effort reports). For others, only the end product matters (a reformatted reference list). | Think about what would happen if someone magically produced the perfect output without doing any of the steps. If that's fine, the task is output-driven. If skipping the steps is a problem (because the steps involve judgment, verification, or professional responsibility), the task needs more oversight. | The boundary shifts depending on context. Drafting a letter might be output-driven internally but process-relevant when it represents institutional policy. |
| **Level 3: Process vs Output** | **Is the Human Thinking the Point?** | Some tasks are valuable precisely because a human applied judgment, expertise, or professional insight. Delegating these to AI doesn't just risk errors. It removes the thing that made the work valuable in the first place. | Ask: "Is someone's professional judgment, creativity, or human perspective what makes this work meaningful?" A PI reviewing effort reports is applying professional knowledge. A mentor writing a recommendation is making a human assessment. A counselor responding to a student is offering empathy. If the value comes from a human thinking about it, AI can support the work but shouldn't replace the thinking. | This principle is easy to misapply. Not every task that a professional does requires their unique insight. A faculty member formatting citations doesn't need their scholarly judgment for that step. Focus on where human cognition is genuinely irreplaceable, not just where a human happens to be doing it currently. |
| **Level 3: Process vs Output** | **Separate What Needs a Trail from What Doesn't** | Even in process-critical workflows, some steps are transparent-by-necessity and others aren't. Identifying which is which lets you use AI where it's safe. | Map your workflow. Which steps need documentation and attestation? Those are transparent-by-necessity. Which steps are supporting work (formatting, summarizing, organizing)? Those may be fine for AI. Example: Budget calculations need a trail; drafting the budget justification narrative may not. | The boundaries between "needs a trail" and "doesn't" aren't always clean. A supporting step can become critical if it contains substantive claims. |
| **Level 3: Process vs Output** | **Match the Level of AI Involvement to the Task** | Different tasks call for different levels of AI involvement. Choosing the right pattern (from "AI assists" to "AI leads") depends on how much human judgment the task requires and how much oversight the stakes demand. | Consider four patterns: **Human leads, AI supports**: you do the core work; AI handles formatting, summarizing, or drafting boilerplate (best for high-transparency tasks). **AI drafts, human verifies thoroughly**: AI produces a first version; you check every claim against source materials (for moderate-transparency tasks). **AI drafts, human spot-checks**: AI does the work; you do a quick reasonableness review (for low-stakes, routine tasks). **Deterministic automation**: a script, formula, or rule-based system handles the task with no AI involved (for tasks needing reproducibility and auditability). Note: even the lightest AI pattern still needs some human oversight, because AI is non-deterministic. It can give different answers each time and fail silently. A Python script on a cron job will produce the same output every time; an AI agent won't. "Fully automated" means something very different for AI than for traditional code. | The boundaries between patterns aren't always clean, and the right pattern may change as you learn how AI performs on a specific task. Start with more oversight and relax as you build confidence. |
| **Level 4: Institutional Context** | **What Would Your Auditor Say?** | A practical gut-check. If an auditor would be uncomfortable with your method, that's a strong signal the task needs more transparency. | Imagine your institution's auditor reviewing this work. Would they ask "how was this done?" and expect a clear, documented answer? If yes, use transparent methods. If they'd only check the final result for accuracy, you have more flexibility with tools. | Auditor expectations vary by institution and by the specific audit. Use this as a directional guide, not a definitive test. |
| **Level 4: Institutional Context** | **Know Your Data Agreement** | Every AI tool has a data processing agreement, a contract that governs what happens to the information you provide. If you don't know what that agreement says, you don't know where your data is going, who can see it, or whether it's being used to train future models. | Before using any AI tool for work, answer: "Does my organization have a data processing agreement with this vendor?" and "Does this agreement cover the type of data I'm about to share?" Many free-tier AI tools explicitly state that user inputs may be used for training, meaning your confidential documents could influence future outputs for anyone. Enterprise agreements typically offer stronger protections, but the specifics vary. Check whether your agreement covers: data retention (how long is it kept?), training exclusion (is your data used to improve the model?), geographic restrictions (where is it processed?), and access controls (who at the vendor can see it?). | Data agreements are legal documents that most users never read. Work with your IT, legal, or compliance team to understand what's covered. Don't assume that "our institution uses this tool" means "I can put anything into it." Approved tools often have approved use cases, and your use case may not be one of them. |
| **Level 4: Institutional Context** | **Does Your Office Have AI Policies?** | Institutional policies may already answer the question for you. If your office has guidelines on AI use, start there before making case-by-case judgments. | Check: Does your institution or department have a policy on using AI for this type of work? Many universities are developing AI use policies that specify which tasks can involve AI assistance. Following existing policy is both easier and safer than making individual calls. | Policies lag behind technology. If no policy exists, you'll need to use judgment, which is what the rest of these principles help with. |
| **Level 4: Institutional Context** | **Consider Who Sees the Final Product** | The audience for the output determines how transparent the process needs to be. Internal working documents have different standards than sponsor deliverables. | Ask: "Who will see or use this output?" Internal brainstorming → more flexibility. Colleague review → moderate transparency. Sponsor submission → high transparency. External publication → consider disclosure requirements. Match your method to your audience's expectations. | Audiences overlap. A draft that starts internal may end up in a sponsor submission. Consider the most demanding potential audience, not just the immediate one. |
| **Level 5: Strategic Positioning** | **Start Transparent and Loosen Up** | When you're unsure, begin with full transparency and relax as you learn. It's easier to loosen oversight than to explain why you skipped it. | Default to process critical or human-in-the-loop for unfamiliar tasks. As you gain experience and understand the accountability requirements, you may find some tasks can move toward output-driven. Starting cautious protects you; starting loose creates risk. | Being overly cautious wastes time. The goal is appropriate transparency, not maximum restriction. Use this when genuinely unsure, not as a default for everything. |
| **Level 5: Strategic Positioning** | **Reassess as Policies Change** | Today's classification won't be permanent. As AI tools improve, institutional policies evolve, and your team gains experience, tasks may shift on the spectrum. | Schedule periodic reviews: "Is this still process critical, or have our tools and policies matured enough to move it to human-in-the-loop?" New regulations may also tighten requirements. Stay current and adjust. | Reclassification needs deliberate review, not quiet drift. Don't let tasks shift zones without explicit discussion with the right people. |
