### How AI Models Work - A Reference Guide for Non-Experts

| Topic | What to Know | Why It Matters | Analogy | Key Resources |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **From Text to Tokens** | AI models don't read words - they break text into "tokens," which are roughly word fragments. "Understanding" becomes two tokens: "under" and "standing." The model works with these fragments as numbers, not as language. Different languages tokenize differently; English is efficient while other languages may use 2-4x more tokens for the same meaning. | Token count determines cost, speed, and how much information fits in a single request. When a tool charges "per token," this is what they mean. When people talk about "context window" limits, they mean token limits. | Imagine translating a book into Morse code before sending it by telegraph. The original meaning is there, but the telegraph doesn't understand English - it just transmits dots and dashes. Tokens are the AI's Morse code. | OpenAI Tokenizer (platform.openai.com/tokenizer) lets you see how text gets split into tokens. |
| **Next-Token Prediction** | At its core, a language model does one thing: given everything it's seen so far, it predicts the most likely next token. It does this one token at a time, thousands of times per response. It doesn't plan ahead, outline its argument, or check its facts. It generates the next word that "fits." | This is why AI can sound perfectly fluent while being completely wrong. Fluency and accuracy are unrelated - the model optimizes for "what word comes next" not "what's true." This is also why longer, more specific prompts help: they constrain the prediction space. | Think of autocomplete on your phone, but trained on billions of pages of text instead of just your message history. It's incredibly good at predicting what plausible text looks like. But "plausible" and "correct" are different things. | |
| **Training vs. Inference** | Training is the expensive part - exposing the model to enormous amounts of text so it learns patterns. This happens once (or occasionally during updates) and costs millions of dollars. Inference is when you actually use the trained model - sending it a prompt and getting a response. Inference is comparatively cheap per query. | Understanding this distinction matters for policy: training data determines what the model "knows" and what biases it carries. The training data has a cutoff date - anything after that date doesn't exist to the model. When people debate AI's environmental impact, training and inference have very different footprints. | Training is like educating a medical student over years: expensive, time-consuming, done once. Inference is like that doctor seeing patients: each appointment uses the training but costs far less than the education did. | |
| **The Knowledge Cutoff** | Models are trained on text up to a specific date. They don't browse the internet, read today's news, or know about recent events unless someone provides that information in the prompt. The model doesn't know what it doesn't know - it will confidently discuss events after its cutoff using patterns from before it. | This is a common source of errors. A model might cite a policy that's been updated, reference an organization that's been renamed, or miss a major development in a field. When accuracy matters, you need to provide current information rather than relying on the model's training. | It's like consulting a very knowledgeable colleague who has been on sabbatical for a year. They're smart and helpful, but they've missed everything that happened while they were away - and they won't tell you they've missed it. | |
| **Attention and Context Windows** | The "context window" is the total amount of text the model can consider at once - your prompt, any documents you include, and the conversation history. Everything in this window competes for the model's attention. Models pay more consistent attention to text at the beginning and end of the window; information in the middle can get less focus. | This directly affects how you work with AI. Long documents may not get fully processed. Important details buried in the middle of a large prompt may be missed. The context window also has a hard limit - exceed it and information gets silently dropped. | Think of the context window as a meeting room with a limited number of seats. Everyone you bring into the room gets to speak, but the more people present, the harder it is for any one voice to be heard. The people who spoke first and most recently tend to be remembered best. | |
| **Hallucination** | "Hallucination" is when a model generates information that is plausible-sounding but fabricated - fake citations, invented statistics, nonexistent people or policies. This isn't a bug being fixed; it's a fundamental property of how prediction works. The model generates text that fits the pattern, whether or not it corresponds to reality. | Never trust AI output on factual claims without verification. This is especially critical in research administration where policy details, regulatory requirements, and funding terms must be exact. Models will cite papers that don't exist, quote regulations with wrong section numbers, and invent institutional policies. | Imagine asking someone to continue a story they've never read. They'll write something that sounds like it belongs, using the right style and vocabulary. But they're creating, not remembering. That's what a model does with facts - it generates what sounds right, not what it looked up. | |
| **"Reasoning" Models** | Some newer models include a "thinking" or "reasoning" step where the model generates an internal chain of thought before responding. This improves performance on logic problems, math, and multi-step tasks. But this isn't reasoning the way humans reason - it's still pattern matching, just with an intermediate step that helps organize the output. | Reasoning models are better for complex, multi-step tasks but cost more (more tokens generated) and take longer. For simple tasks, a standard model may be faster and cheaper. The "thinking" trace can also provide transparency into how the model approached a problem, which supports auditability. | It's the difference between answering a math question off the top of your head versus writing out your work on scratch paper first. The scratch paper doesn't guarantee the right answer, but it helps organize the approach and makes it easier to spot mistakes. | |
| **Open vs. Closed Models** | "Closed" models (like GPT-4, Claude, Gemini) are hosted by companies; you send data to their servers and get responses back. "Open" models (like Llama, Mistral) can be downloaded and run on your own hardware. Open models give you more control over data but require technical infrastructure. | For research involving sensitive data, where data goes matters. Closed models mean your prompts travel to external servers. Open models can run locally, keeping data in-house - but require IT resources and expertise. Your institution's data policies should guide this choice. | Closed models are like using a commercial lab: convenient and well-maintained, but your samples leave your building. Open models are like building your own lab: you control everything, but you need the space, equipment, and staff to run it. | |
| **Where Your Data Goes** | When you use a commercial AI service, your prompt is sent to remote servers for processing. Policies on data retention, training use, and access vary by provider and plan. Free tiers often have weaker data protections than enterprise agreements. API access typically has different (often better) data terms than web chat interfaces. | Research administrators should know their institution's AI data agreements. Is the institution on an enterprise plan? Does the plan prohibit using prompts for training? Are there specific AI tools that have been vetted for sensitive (but not restricted) data? These answers determine which tools are appropriate for which tasks. | When you mail a letter, you trust the postal service not to read it - but the policy matters. Some AI services are like certified mail (tracked, protected, contractual guarantees). Others are like dropping a postcard in a public mailbox. Know which one you're using. | Your institution's IT security office should have current AI data handling policies. Also see provider-specific data policies: OpenAI Enterprise (openai.com/enterprise-privacy), Google Workspace AI (cloud.google.com/terms), Anthropic usage policy (anthropic.com/policies). |
| **Environmental Impact: Training** | Training large AI models requires enormous computational resources. Training GPT-4-scale models is estimated to consume thousands of megawatt-hours of electricity. This also requires significant water for cooling data centers. However, training happens once - or infrequently during updates - not every time someone uses the model. | The environmental conversation should distinguish between training (massive one-time cost) and inference (small per-query cost that scales with usage). Institutions considering sustainability should factor AI use into their environmental commitments, but with accurate framing. | Training a large model is like constructing a building: enormous upfront resource cost, but once built, each person who uses it adds only a little to the energy bill. Training a GPT-4-scale model uses roughly the same energy as 100 American homes use in an entire year. But that training produces a tool that millions of people then use. | IEA (2024) "Electricity 2024" report on data center energy (iea.org/reports/electricity-2024). Strubell et al. (2019) "Energy and Policy Considerations for Deep Learning in NLP." Patterson et al. (2021) "Carbon Emissions and Large Neural Network Training." |
| **Environmental Impact: Inference** | A single AI query uses roughly the energy of leaving a LED light bulb on for 5-15 minutes. That's small individually. But if your institution has 5,000 employees each making 20 AI queries a day, that's the equivalent of running about 100 household refrigerators around the clock. The longer and more complex the query (especially with reasoning models), the more energy it uses. | For individual users, the per-query cost is genuinely small - comparable to streaming a few minutes of video. For institutions rolling out AI broadly, the aggregate energy impact is worth tracking. This is particularly relevant for institutions with sustainability commitments or carbon reduction targets. | Think of each AI query like leaving a light on in a room for a few minutes. One light, one time - no big deal. But a building where every room has the lights on all day adds up to a real electricity bill. | IEA (2024) data center projections. de Vries (2023) "The growing energy footprint of artificial intelligence" in Joule. Your institution's sustainability office may have tools for estimating digital carbon footprints. |
| **Bias in Training Data** | Models learn patterns from their training data, including biases present in that data. If the training corpus over-represents certain perspectives, demographics, or writing styles, the model's outputs will reflect those skews. This isn't a simple problem to fix - bias can be subtle, contextual, and domain-specific. | For research contexts, this means AI output may reflect dominant cultural assumptions, underrepresent minority perspectives, or reproduce historical biases in language and framing. Anyone using AI for policy drafting, communications, or analysis should be aware that the tool's "neutral" output isn't actually neutral. | A model trained mostly on English-language academic text will write like an English-speaking academic - even when that voice isn't appropriate for the audience or context. It's not choosing that voice deliberately; it's the only voice it knows well. | NIST AI 100-2 (2024) "Towards a Standard for Identifying and Managing Bias in Artificial Intelligence." |
