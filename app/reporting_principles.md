### Reporting Principles

| Advice Category | Principle | Why This Works | What It Is | Limitations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Level 1: Basic Disclosure** | **State What the AI Did** | The simplest and most important form of transparency: telling people that AI was involved and what role it played. Without this, everything else is invisible. | At minimum, document: (1) that AI was used, (2) which model or tool, (3) what task it performed, and (4) what the human did with the output. Example: "GPT-4 was used to generate a first draft of the executive summary. The author revised for accuracy and tone." This takes one sentence and transforms hidden AI use into transparent practice. | Stating what the AI did doesn't prove the work is good; it just makes the process visible. Pair with evaluation documentation for full accountability. |
| **Level 1: Basic Disclosure** | **Disclose Early, Not After the Fact** | Disclosing AI use after someone discovers it erodes trust far more than disclosing upfront. Proactive transparency builds credibility; reactive disclosure looks like a cover-up. | Make AI disclosure a default part of your workflow, not something you add when asked. Include it in drafts, submissions, and internal communications from the start. If you're unsure whether disclosure is required, disclose anyway. The cost of unnecessary transparency is near zero, while the cost of appearing to hide AI use can be significant. | Some contexts may not have clear norms about AI disclosure yet. When norms are unclear, lead by example. Your transparency helps establish the standard. |
| **Level 1: Basic Disclosure** | **Match Disclosure to Audience** | Different audiences need different levels of detail. A research paper needs a methods section; a Slack message needs a quick note; a regulatory submission needs a full audit trail. | Calibrate your disclosure: **Casual** (internal, low-stakes): "I used AI to help draft this." **Professional** (external, moderate-stakes): "This summary was drafted with [model] and reviewed by [person] for accuracy." **Formal** (regulatory, high-stakes): Full methodology section with model, prompt, context, evaluation criteria, and human review process. | It's easy to under-disclose for convenience. When in doubt, err toward more detail. You can always simplify later, but you can't add transparency retroactively. |
| **Level 2: Process Documentation** | **Document the Prompt** | The prompt is the most direct record of what you asked the AI to do. Without it, no one can evaluate whether the output was reasonable given the input. | Save your prompt, or at least a paraphrase of it. For important work, save the exact prompt. For routine work, a summary is fine: "I asked the model to summarize the key findings from the attached report in 3 bullet points." This allows others to understand, replicate, or improve your approach. | Prompts can be long and complex, especially in multi-turn conversations. For extended interactions, document the key prompts and turning points rather than every message. |
| **Level 2: Process Documentation** | **Document the Context You Provided** | What the AI knows depends entirely on what you gave it. Without recording what context was included, no one can assess whether the output was well-informed or based on incomplete information. | Note what documents, data, or examples you provided to the AI, and what you deliberately excluded. Example: "The model was given the 2024 policy document (v3.2) and last quarter's budget summary. It was not given the pending amendment or internal memos." This lets others evaluate whether the AI had adequate information. | Tracking every piece of context is burdensome for routine tasks. Focus documentation effort on tasks where context quality significantly affects output quality. |
| **Level 2: Process Documentation** | **Record What You Changed** | The gap between what the AI produced and what you published is where human judgment lives. Documenting your changes shows that you engaged critically with the output rather than accepting it blindly. | Keep track of significant changes you made to AI output: corrections, additions, removals, and restructuring. You don't need a line-by-line diff. A summary is fine: "I corrected two citations, added the institutional context paragraph, and removed a hallucinated statistic." This demonstrates active human oversight. | Tracking changes is easy when they're major but tedious when they're numerous small edits. For heavily edited output, a general statement about the nature and extent of changes may be sufficient. |
| **Level 2: Process Documentation** | **Document What You Got** | Waiting for a complete evaluation before documenting anything means the documentation often never happens. Partial documentation is vastly more useful than none. | Report everything you can, even if some steps are incomplete. If you know the task, model, and prompt but haven't done a formal accuracy check, document the first three and note that evaluation is pending. A TaMPER report with gaps is still more transparent than no report at all. The reporting step depends on the others (Task, Model, Prompt, Evaluation), but it shouldn't be blocked by them. | This can create a false sense of completeness. Be honest about what's missing: "Evaluation: not yet performed" is more transparent than silently omitting the section. Readers need to know the difference between "we checked and it's fine" and "we haven't checked yet." |
| **Level 3: Formal Reporting** | **Use a Reporting Framework** | Frameworks like TaMPER (Task, Model, Prompt, Evaluation, Reporting) provide a consistent structure for AI methodology sections, ensuring nothing important is omitted. | For formal work, organize your documentation around a framework: **Task** (what you were trying to do), **Model** (what AI tool you used), **Prompt** (what you asked), **Evaluation** (how you checked the output), **Reporting** (this documentation itself). This structure works for methods sections, appendices, or internal documentation. | Frameworks can feel like overhead for simple tasks. Use them for work that will be reviewed, published, or audited, not for every email draft. |
| **Level 3: Formal Reporting** | **Include Negative Results** | Documenting what didn't work is as valuable as documenting what did. Failed approaches, rejected outputs, and abandoned strategies help others avoid the same dead ends. | Note when AI output was rejected and why: "The initial draft hallucinated three citations; these were removed and replaced with verified sources." Or: "We initially tried using AI for the statistical analysis but found the results unreliable; we reverted to manual calculation." This honesty strengthens trust in what you did use. | Reporting failures can feel uncomfortable, especially in formal contexts. Frame negative results as evidence of rigor. They show you didn't accept everything uncritically. |
| **Level 3: Formal Reporting** | **Version Your Methodology** | AI tools, models, and capabilities change rapidly. What works today may not work (or may work differently) tomorrow. Versioning your methodology ensures reproducibility. | Record the specific model version, date of use, and any relevant settings (temperature, system prompt, etc.). Example: "Generated using GPT-4-turbo (March 2024) with default settings." This allows others to reproduce your results or understand why their results differ when using a newer model. | Model versions are not always visible to end users, and API behavior can change without notice. Document what you can, and note any uncertainty about the exact configuration. |
| **Level 4: Institutional Standards** | **Establish Team Documentation Norms** | Individual documentation practices vary widely. Team-level standards ensure consistency, make onboarding easier, and create a shared expectation of transparency. | Work with your team to define minimum documentation requirements for AI-assisted work: what must always be disclosed, what format to use, where to store documentation, and who reviews it. Even a simple checklist ("Model used? Prompt saved? Output reviewed?") creates accountability. | Standards that are too rigid get ignored; standards that are too vague get interpreted inconsistently. Start simple, enforce consistently, and iterate based on experience. |
| **Level 4: Institutional Standards** | **Create Documentation Templates** | Templates lower the barrier to good documentation by removing the "blank page" problem. When the structure is pre-built, users just fill in the details. | Develop templates for common AI documentation scenarios: a methods paragraph template, a disclosure statement template, an internal review checklist. Customize for your organization's needs and share widely. | Templates must be maintained and updated as practices evolve. Assign ownership and review them periodically. Outdated templates are worse than no templates. |
| **Level 5: Meta-Reporting** | **Review Your Documentation Practices** | Documentation practices that made sense six months ago may not fit current workflows, tools, or institutional requirements. Regular review prevents documentation debt. | Periodically ask: "Is our documentation capturing what matters? Are we documenting too much, too little, or the wrong things? Has our toolset changed in ways that require updated documentation practices?" Use this review to simplify, update, or expand your approach. | Meta-review can feel like unnecessary overhead. Schedule it infrequently (quarterly or after major workflow changes) and keep it focused on practical improvements. |
| **Level 5: Meta-Reporting** | **Documentation as Learning** | Good documentation isn't just for accountability. It's a learning resource. Reviewing how you've used AI over time reveals patterns, improvements, and persistent challenges. | Review your documentation archive periodically: "What types of tasks does AI help with most? Where do I consistently need to make corrections? How has my prompting improved?" This reflection accelerates your own learning and provides evidence for institutional AI strategy decisions. | This requires maintaining accessible, organized documentation over time, which many teams don't do. Even informal notes in a shared document are better than nothing. |
