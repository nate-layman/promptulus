### Evaluation Principles

| Advice Category | Principle | Why This Works | What It Is | Limitations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Level 1: Foundational Checks** | **Check Facts, Not Just Fluency** | AI produces grammatically perfect, confident-sounding text regardless of whether the content is true. Fluent writing creates a false sense of accuracy that catches even experienced users off guard. | Read AI output like a fact-checker, not a proofreader. For every specific claim — numbers, dates, citations, proper nouns, statistics — verify against an authoritative source. Ask: "Is this actually true, or does it just sound true?" The more confident the AI sounds, the more carefully you should check. | Fact-checking every claim is time-consuming and impractical for large outputs. Prioritize checking the claims that matter most for your task — high-stakes numbers, citations, and anything that will be shared externally. |
| **Level 1: Foundational Checks** | **Watch for Hallucinations** | AI models generate text by predicting likely next words, which means they can fabricate plausible-sounding information — fake citations, nonexistent studies, invented statistics — with complete confidence. | Be especially suspicious of: specific citations or references (verify they exist), precise numbers or statistics (verify the source), named individuals or organizations (verify the association), and historical claims (verify the facts). If you can't verify a claim, treat it as unverified — not as true. | Hallucinations can be subtle — the AI might cite a real author but assign them a paper they never wrote, or give a real statistic from the wrong year. Surface-level verification isn't always enough. |
| **Level 1: Foundational Checks** | **Don't Confuse Quantity with Quality** | AI can generate large volumes of text quickly, which creates a perception of thoroughness. But more output doesn't mean better output — it may mean more errors spread across more text. | Before evaluating AI output, ask: "Did I ask for this much?" If the output is longer or more detailed than necessary, the excess may contain filler, redundancy, or fabricated details added to fill space. Trim to what you actually need and evaluate that. | Sometimes length is appropriate — a thorough analysis should be thorough. The principle is about evaluating whether the length serves the task or just looks impressive. |
| **Level 2: Systematic Verification** | **Test with Known Answers** | The most reliable way to calibrate AI accuracy is to give it a question you already know the answer to. If it gets that wrong, you can't trust it on questions where you don't know the answer. | Before relying on AI for a task, test it with a similar task where you know the correct output. If it produces your institution's grant deadline correctly when you provide the policy document, you can have more confidence in its other extractions from that document. If it gets the known answer wrong, recalibrate your trust. | Known-answer tests only validate performance on similar questions. AI might ace your test case and fail on the actual task if the actual task is harder, more nuanced, or further from its training data. |
| **Level 2: Systematic Verification** | **Check the Edges, Not Just the Center** | AI performs best on common, well-represented scenarios and worst on edge cases, unusual situations, and boundary conditions. Testing only typical cases gives a false sense of reliability. | After getting AI output, ask: "What's the weirdest or most unusual case this output needs to handle?" Test those specifically. If you asked AI to categorize tasks, try it with an ambiguous task that could go either way. If it wrote a policy summary, check whether it handled the exceptions and caveats correctly. | Identifying meaningful edge cases requires domain expertise. You need to know enough about the subject to predict where the AI is most likely to fail — which is exactly the expertise the user may be hoping AI can replace. |
| **Level 2: Systematic Verification** | **Compare Multiple Outputs** | A single AI response may contain errors that only become visible when compared to alternative responses. Running the same prompt multiple times or using different models reveals inconsistencies. | Run your prompt 2-3 times and compare the outputs. Where they agree, you can be more confident. Where they disagree, investigate — the disagreement highlights areas of uncertainty. You can also try the same prompt on a different model to see if the answer is consistent across systems. | Multiple runs cost more time and API calls. Reserve this technique for high-stakes outputs where accuracy matters significantly. For low-stakes tasks, a single output with basic verification may suffice. |
| **Level 3: Reliability & Consistency** | **Test Reproducibility** | If an AI produces different answers to the same question on different occasions, the output is unreliable — you got lucky or unlucky, not a trustworthy result. | Run the same prompt multiple times with the same context. If the AI gives substantially different answers each time (different conclusions, contradictory claims, varying numbers), the output isn't reliable enough to trust. Consistent answers across runs are more trustworthy than a single response, even if they're not guaranteed to be correct. | AI models have inherent randomness (temperature). Small variations in wording are normal and don't indicate unreliability. Focus on whether the substance — conclusions, numbers, recommendations — remains consistent. |
| **Level 3: Reliability & Consistency** | **Assess Reasoning, Not Just Conclusions** | A correct conclusion reached through flawed reasoning is a lucky accident — it won't hold up when conditions change. Evaluating the reasoning catches errors that evaluating only the conclusion would miss. | When AI provides an analysis or recommendation, trace the logic: "Does each step follow from the previous one? Are the assumptions stated and reasonable? Would different assumptions lead to a different conclusion?" A well-reasoned wrong answer is easier to fix than a right answer with hidden flaws. | Evaluating reasoning requires subject matter expertise and takes more time than checking conclusions. For routine, low-stakes tasks, checking the conclusion alone may be sufficient. |
| **Level 3: Reliability & Consistency** | **Watch for Sycophancy** | AI models are trained to be helpful and agreeable, which means they sometimes tell you what you want to hear rather than what's true. They may agree with your framing even when it's wrong. | Be suspicious when the AI enthusiastically agrees with everything you say, especially if you've stated an opinion or made an assumption in your prompt. Test by deliberately including a wrong assumption and seeing if the AI corrects you or goes along with it. If it agrees with your errors, its other agreements are less trustworthy. | Sycophancy is hard to detect because agreement feels good. You may not notice the AI is agreeing with you rather than thinking independently. Having a colleague review the interaction can help catch this pattern. |
| **Level 4: Workflow Integration** | **Build Evaluation into the Process** | Evaluation done as an afterthought is often skipped or rushed. Building it into the workflow from the start ensures it actually happens. | Before you start using AI for a task, decide how you'll evaluate the output. Write it down: "I will check all citations against Google Scholar," or "I will verify all numbers against the original spreadsheet," or "I will have a colleague review the final draft." Make evaluation a step in your process, not an optional add-on. | Pre-planning evaluation requires knowing your failure modes before you see the output. Start with a basic plan and refine it after you see what kinds of errors actually occur. |
| **Level 4: Workflow Integration** | **Match Evaluation Rigor to Stakes** | Not every AI output needs the same level of scrutiny. Over-evaluating low-stakes work wastes time; under-evaluating high-stakes work creates risk. | Classify your output: **High stakes** (going to external audiences, regulatory bodies, or affecting decisions) → thorough fact-checking, multiple verification methods. **Medium stakes** (internal use, informing but not determining decisions) → spot-checking key claims. **Low stakes** (brainstorming, exploration, personal use) → basic reasonableness check. | The line between stakes levels is often fuzzy. When in doubt, err on the side of more evaluation rather than less — the cost of checking is almost always less than the cost of publishing an error. |
| **Level 5: Meta-Evaluation** | **Track Your Trust Calibration** | Your trust in AI should be earned through experience, not assumed. Tracking where AI succeeds and fails on your specific tasks helps you calibrate appropriate trust over time. | Keep a simple log: "Task type → AI accuracy → What I had to fix." Over time, patterns emerge: maybe AI is great at formatting but terrible at numbers, or excellent at summaries but unreliable for original analysis. Use this data to decide where AI helps and where it hurts. | Maintaining a log requires discipline and adds overhead. Even a rough mental model ("AI is usually wrong about dates in our context") is better than no calibration at all. |
| **Level 5: Meta-Evaluation** | **Question Your Own Evaluation** | Your evaluation of AI output is itself subject to biases — confirmation bias (seeing what you expect), automation bias (trusting the machine), and anchoring (being influenced by the AI's confident framing). | Periodically ask: "Am I really checking this, or am I just confirming what the AI said?" Try to evaluate the output before reading the AI's explanation, so you form your own judgment first. Have someone unfamiliar with the AI's output review it fresh — they'll catch things your primed perspective misses. | Self-awareness about evaluation biases is valuable but not sufficient. External review from someone who hasn't seen the AI output is the most reliable check against your own blind spots. |
