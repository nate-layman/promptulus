### Evaluation Principles

| Advice Category | Principle | Why This Works | What It Is | Limitations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Level 1: Foundational Checks** | **Check Facts, Not Just Fluency** | AI produces grammatically perfect, confident-sounding text regardless of whether the content is true. Fluent writing creates a false sense of accuracy that catches even experienced users off guard. | Read AI output like a fact-checker, not a proofreader. For every specific claim — numbers, dates, citations, proper nouns, statistics — verify against an authoritative source. Ask: "Is this actually true, or does it just sound true?" The more confident the AI sounds, the more carefully you should check. | Fact-checking every claim is time-consuming and impractical for large outputs. Prioritize checking the claims that matter most for your task — high-stakes numbers, citations, and anything that will be shared externally. |
| **Level 1: Foundational Checks** | **Watch for Hallucinations** | AI models generate text by predicting likely next words, which means they can fabricate plausible-sounding information with complete confidence. The output reads as if the AI knows — but it's generating probable text, not retrieving verified facts. | Hallucination means the AI invented something that sounds real but isn't. Be suspicious of any specific claim: precise numbers, named individuals, dates, or statistics. The more specific and confident the claim, the more important it is to verify. If you can't verify a claim from an authoritative source, treat it as unverified — not as true. The AI will never tell you it's guessing. | Hallucinations can be subtle — the AI might mix real and fabricated details, making the output harder to evaluate than if it were entirely wrong. You need domain knowledge to spot the seams. |
| **Level 1: Foundational Checks** | **Know the Types of AI Errors** | When you can name the type of error, you know where to look for it. Different error types require different verification strategies — catching a hallucination is a different skill than catching an omission. | AI makes several distinct kinds of mistakes: **Hallucinations** — fabricating information that sounds real (fake citations, invented statistics). **False positives** — claiming something is true or relevant when it isn't (flagging a compliant document as non-compliant). **False negatives** — missing something that should have been caught (overlooking a key requirement). **Omissions** — leaving out important information without telling you. **Confident errors** — stating wrong information with no hedging or uncertainty. After reviewing AI output, run through each error type and ask: "Could this have happened here?" | Knowing error types doesn't guarantee you'll catch them — you still need domain knowledge to recognize when a hallucination looks plausible or when something important was omitted. This principle gives you a checklist, not a guarantee. |
| **Level 1: Foundational Checks** | **Check What's Missing, Not Just What's There** | AI fails silently. It won't tell you it skipped a requirement, omitted a key point, or quietly dropped something from your instructions. If you only check what the AI produced, you'll miss what it didn't produce. | After reviewing AI output, go back to your original request and check off each requirement: "Did I ask for five sections? Are there five?" "Did I ask it to address budget, timeline, and personnel? Are all three covered?" AI often produces polished, complete-looking output that quietly drops the hardest parts of your request. The more complex your request, the more likely something was silently omitted. | Checking for omissions requires you to have a clear sense of what should be there — which means you need a clear request in the first place. If your original prompt was vague, you won't know what's missing. This principle works best when paired with a specific, structured prompt. |
| **Level 1: Foundational Checks** | **Don't Confuse Quantity with Quality** | AI can generate large volumes of text quickly, which creates a perception of thoroughness. But more output doesn't mean better output — it may mean more errors spread across more text. | Before evaluating AI output, ask: "Did I ask for this much?" If the output is longer or more detailed than necessary, the excess may contain filler, redundancy, or fabricated details added to fill space. Trim to what you actually need and evaluate that. | Sometimes length is appropriate — a thorough analysis should be thorough. The principle is about evaluating whether the length serves the task or just looks impressive. |
| **Level 2: Systematic Verification** | **Test with Known Answers** | The most reliable way to calibrate AI accuracy is to give it a question you already know the answer to. If it gets that wrong, you can't trust it on questions where you don't know the answer. | Before relying on AI for a task, test it with a similar task where you know the correct output. If it produces your institution's grant deadline correctly when you provide the policy document, you can have more confidence in its other extractions from that document. If it gets the known answer wrong, recalibrate your trust. | Known-answer tests only validate performance on similar questions. AI might ace your test case and fail on the actual task if the actual task is harder, more nuanced, or further from its training data. |
| **Level 2: Systematic Verification** | **Check the Edges, Not Just the Center** | AI performs best on common, well-represented scenarios and worst on edge cases, unusual situations, and boundary conditions. Testing only typical cases gives a false sense of reliability. | After getting AI output, ask: "What's the weirdest or most unusual case this output needs to handle?" Test those specifically. If you asked AI to categorize tasks, try it with an ambiguous task that could go either way. If it wrote a policy summary, check whether it handled the exceptions and caveats correctly. | Identifying meaningful edge cases requires domain expertise. You need to know enough about the subject to predict where the AI is most likely to fail — which is exactly the expertise the user may be hoping AI can replace. |
| **Level 2: Systematic Verification** | **Verify Sources Actually Exist** | AI fabricates citations with perfect formatting — real-looking author names, plausible journal titles, correct-seeming DOIs that lead nowhere. A beautifully formatted reference list is not evidence that the sources are real. | For every citation, reference, or source the AI provides: look it up. Search for the exact title in Google Scholar, PubMed, or the relevant database. Check that the author actually wrote it, that it was published where the AI says it was, and that it says what the AI claims. A common AI trick is to cite a real author with a fabricated paper, or cite a real paper but misrepresent its findings. Don't just check that the reference *exists* — check that it says what the AI *claims* it says. | This is most critical for academic and regulatory work where citations carry weight. For informal summaries or brainstorming, citation verification may be overkill. Prioritize verification for any output that will be published, submitted, or used to justify a decision. |
| **Level 2: Systematic Verification** | **Compare Output Against Your Inputs** | When you give AI source documents — policies, spreadsheets, reports — you expect it to use them faithfully. But AI can paraphrase inaccurately, introduce claims not in your sources, or subtly change meaning while sounding correct. The output may read well without actually reflecting your data. | After getting AI output, go back to the documents you provided and cross-reference. Did the AI accurately represent the numbers from your spreadsheet? Did it correctly summarize the policy language? Did it introduce any claims that aren't in your source material? Read with your source document open side-by-side and check each substantive claim against the original. | This is time-consuming for long outputs but essential when accuracy matters. For high-stakes work, check everything against the source. For lower stakes, spot-check the most important claims and any numbers or specific language. |
| **Level 3: Reliability & Consistency** | **Test for Consistency** | A single AI response can look perfect. Run it again and you might get a completely different answer — or the same errors might become visible by comparison. If the AI isn't consistent with itself, you can't trust any single output. | Run your prompt 2-3 times with the same context and compare the outputs. Where they agree, you can be more confident. Where they disagree, investigate — the disagreement reveals uncertainty the AI didn't flag. Focus on whether the substance changes (conclusions, numbers, recommendations), not just the wording — small phrasing variations are normal, but different conclusions are a red flag. For high-stakes tasks, also try a different model or rephrase your prompt to see if the answer holds up across conditions, not just across runs. | Multiple runs cost time. Reserve this for outputs where accuracy matters. For low-stakes tasks, a single output with basic fact-checking may suffice. Note that consistency doesn't guarantee correctness — the AI can be consistently wrong. |
| **Level 3: Reliability & Consistency** | **Assess Reasoning, Not Just Conclusions** | A correct conclusion reached through flawed reasoning is a lucky accident — it won't hold up when conditions change. Evaluating the reasoning catches errors that evaluating only the conclusion would miss. | When AI provides an analysis or recommendation, trace the logic: "Does each step follow from the previous one? Are the assumptions stated and reasonable? Would different assumptions lead to a different conclusion?" A well-reasoned wrong answer is easier to fix than a right answer with hidden flaws. | Evaluating reasoning requires subject matter expertise and takes more time than checking conclusions. For routine, low-stakes tasks, checking the conclusion alone may be sufficient. |
| **Level 3: Reliability & Consistency** | **Test for Prompt Brittleness** | If slightly rephrasing your prompt produces a completely different answer, the AI's output depends more on your wording than on the underlying truth. Reliable answers should survive small changes in how you ask. | Try rephrasing your prompt — change the word order, use synonyms, ask the same question from a different angle. If the AI gives substantially different answers to slightly different versions of the same question, the output is brittle and shouldn't be trusted. A robust answer holds up regardless of how you phrase the question. This is especially important for classification tasks, policy interpretations, or any task where the "right answer" shouldn't depend on your exact wording. | Some variation across phrasings is normal — AI may emphasize different aspects or organize information differently. Focus on whether the substance changes, not the style. If the conclusion flips because you used "evaluate" instead of "assess," that's brittleness. If the same conclusion is just phrased differently, that's fine. |
| **Level 3: Reliability & Consistency** | **Watch for Sycophancy** | AI models are trained to be helpful and agreeable, which means they sometimes tell you what you want to hear rather than what's true. They may agree with your framing even when it's wrong. | Be suspicious when the AI enthusiastically agrees with everything you say, especially if you've stated an opinion or made an assumption in your prompt. Test by deliberately including a wrong assumption and seeing if the AI corrects you or goes along with it. If it agrees with your errors, its other agreements are less trustworthy. | Sycophancy is hard to detect because agreement feels good. You may not notice the AI is agreeing with you rather than thinking independently. Having a colleague review the interaction can help catch this pattern. |
| **Level 4: Workflow Integration** | **Check Output for Sensitive Data Leakage** | AI can surface sensitive information in unexpected ways — echoing PII from your context, inferring private details, or including confidential data in a summary you planned to share externally. If you don't check for this before sharing, you may accidentally disclose information you intended to keep private. | Before sharing or publishing AI output, scan for: **Names and identifiers** that should have been anonymized, **confidential details** from your source documents that shouldn't appear in the final product, **inferred information** the AI may have filled in (e.g., guessing someone's identity from contextual clues), and **internal jargon or references** that reveal more about your organization than intended. This is especially important when the output will be shared externally — what's routine context internally may be a privacy violation externally. | This check adds time but is non-negotiable for outputs that will be seen by anyone outside your immediate team. For internal-only, low-sensitivity outputs, a quick scan may suffice. Build the habit now so it's automatic when the stakes are high. |
| **Level 4: Workflow Integration** | **Build Evaluation into the Process** | Evaluation done as an afterthought is often skipped or rushed. Building it into the workflow from the start ensures it actually happens. | Before you start using AI for a task, decide how you'll evaluate the output. Write it down: "I will check all citations against Google Scholar," or "I will verify all numbers against the original spreadsheet," or "I will have a colleague review the final draft." Make evaluation a step in your process, not an optional add-on. | Pre-planning evaluation requires knowing your failure modes before you see the output. Start with a basic plan and refine it after you see what kinds of errors actually occur. |
| **Level 4: Workflow Integration** | **Match Evaluation Rigor to Stakes** | Not every AI output needs the same level of scrutiny. Over-evaluating low-stakes work wastes time; under-evaluating high-stakes work creates risk. | Classify your output: **High stakes** (going to external audiences, regulatory bodies, or affecting decisions) → thorough fact-checking, multiple verification methods. **Medium stakes** (internal use, informing but not determining decisions) → spot-checking key claims. **Low stakes** (brainstorming, exploration, personal use) → basic reasonableness check. | The line between stakes levels is often fuzzy. When in doubt, err on the side of more evaluation rather than less — the cost of checking is almost always less than the cost of publishing an error. |
| **Level 4: Workflow Integration** | **Test Like a Scientist** | Casual spot-checking catches obvious errors but misses systematic ones. To really know whether AI output is reliable, you need to evaluate it like an experiment: define your test conditions, choose your evaluation method, and classify the errors. This is the difference between "it looked okay" and "I measured its accuracy." | Follow the testing methodology: **1. Record the model and prompt** — you can't evaluate what you can't reproduce. Note which model, what version, and exactly what you asked (visit Clarion for help documenting this). **2. Run replicates** — use the Test for Consistency principle (Level 3) to check if the AI gives stable answers. **3. Evaluate systematically** — for each output, classify what went right and wrong. For clear-cut tasks (did it extract the correct date? did it classify this correctly?), use **binary evaluation**: yes/no, success/fail, correct/incorrect. For nuanced tasks where quality exists on a spectrum (how good is this summary? how appropriate is this tone?), use a **Likert scale** with descriptive labels. Define the dimensions that matter for your specific task (e.g., accuracy, completeness, tone, relevance — whatever "good" means for this output). Then choose your scale: a **5-point scale** (1 = completely wrong, 2 = mostly wrong, 3 = mixed, 4 = mostly right, 5 = fully accurate) gives you a neutral midpoint for ambiguous cases. A **4-point scale** (1 = unacceptable, 2 = needs significant revision, 3 = minor issues, 4 = ready to use) forces a decision — there's no safe middle ground, which is useful when you need a clear accept/reject signal. Use descriptive labels rather than bare numbers — labels force you to think about what the rating actually means. **Tip: start small** — test on a sample of 3-5 cases before applying to your full task. If the AI fails on the sample, you've saved yourself the time of running the whole thing. **4. Classify your errors** — specifically look for **false positives** (AI says something is true/relevant when it isn't) and **false negatives** (AI misses something it should have caught). These tell you not just whether the AI is wrong, but *how* it's wrong — which determines whether you can correct for its weaknesses or need a different approach entirely. | This level of rigor isn't needed for every task — it's most valuable for tasks you'll repeat, where you're building trust in a workflow, or where the stakes are high enough to justify the effort. For one-off, low-stakes tasks, lighter evaluation methods are fine. |
| **Level 5: Meta-Evaluation** | **Track Your Trust Calibration** | Your trust in AI should be earned through experience, not assumed. Tracking where AI succeeds and fails on your specific tasks helps you calibrate appropriate trust over time. | Keep a simple log: "Task type → AI accuracy → What I had to fix." Over time, patterns emerge: maybe AI is great at formatting but terrible at numbers, or excellent at summaries but unreliable for original analysis. Use this data to decide where AI helps and where it hurts. | Maintaining a log requires discipline and adds overhead. Even a rough mental model ("AI is usually wrong about dates in our context") is better than no calibration at all. |
| **Level 5: Meta-Evaluation** | **Question Your Own Evaluation** | Your evaluation of AI output is itself subject to biases — confirmation bias (seeing what you expect), automation bias (trusting the machine), and anchoring (being influenced by the AI's confident framing). | Periodically ask: "Am I really checking this, or am I just confirming what the AI said?" Try to evaluate the output before reading the AI's explanation, so you form your own judgment first. Have someone unfamiliar with the AI's output review it fresh — they'll catch things your primed perspective misses. | Self-awareness about evaluation biases is valuable but not sufficient. External review from someone who hasn't seen the AI output is the most reliable check against your own blind spots. |
